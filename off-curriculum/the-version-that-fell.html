The Version That Fell.

On being misread by people who only saw us at the worst point, and the quiet cost of trying to update their picture.

A note ~5 min read

Most of the people who have known us are still walking around with an outdated picture of who we are.

They saw us at a particular moment, sometimes a hard one. A loss, a stumble, a public silence after something broke. That version, the one at the worst point, is the one they kept. The work that came after was private. The change was internal. They were not there for any of it.

Most of us know this from the inside. We can feel it when we walk into a room of people from before. There is a small, particular sting in being introduced to ourselves through someone else’s outdated impression. We are not who they remember. We have been working on something for a long time, and they have no way of knowing.

The first instinct is usually to correct them... to say something, to send something, to find an opportunity to update the picture. To prove that we have changed. The instinct is honest, but it almost never lands.

The reason is simple. The picture they carry of us is not really about us. It is about them, about the moment they were in when they last paid attention, about what was easier to believe than to keep updating. People are not constantly revising their files on other people. They keep the version that fits the slot.

If we try to argue with the picture, we end up confirming it. We sound defensive. We look like the version that needs them to see something. The version they remember was a version that still needed something from them. The version we have become does not. Trying to explain it puts us back in the old position.

So the move, the one that takes the longest to learn but eventually becomes obvious, is this. Let the picture stay outdated. Do not fight it. Do not perform around it. Do not stage a comeback so they can finally see. Just keep doing the work. The proof shows up on its own time, and when it does, the picture updates on its own. Or it does not, and we discover that we no longer needed it to.

Marcus Aurelius noticed something like this two thousand years ago. He was running an empire and had every excuse to spend his attention on what other people thought of him. He chose not to:

It never ceases to amaze me: we all love ourselves more than other people, but care more about their opinion than our own. Marcus Aurelius · Meditations XII.4 · c. 170-180 CE · Hays translation

The point of his sentence is the gap. We are the only ones inside our own lives. Everyone else is reading us from outside. Their picture is partial, delayed, filtered through whatever else they were dealing with at the time they last looked. None of that is theirs to fix. None of it is ours either.

There is something else worth saying. The same is true in the other direction.

Think of the friend we lost touch with, the family member we cannot quite forgive, the colleague who let us down, the person who hurt us. The version of them we are working from is also outdated. They have been doing their own work, or not, and we are not there for any of it either. Our picture of them is just as partial and just as delayed.

That recognition cuts both ways. It frees us from caring as much about the pictures other people carry of us. It also reminds us to hold the pictures we carry of others a little more lightly.

What we get back, when we stop trying to update the picture, is the time and attention we used to spend on it. That is a lot of time and attention. The mind that used to rehearse explanations gets quiet. The energy that used to go into proving the new version was real goes back into actually building it. The proof becomes the work itself, not the announcement of it.

The people who matter eventually see it, or some of them do. The people who do not see it were not paying close enough attention to notice the rebuild anyway. Both groups end up in the right place.

If you have arrived here knowing that someone, somewhere, still has you frozen at the worst version of yourself, this is what I would say. Let them. The picture is theirs to carry. It is not yours to update. Keep doing the work. The version they last saw is already gone. The one taking the work seriously is the only one that matters.

One day at a time.

Easy does it.

Do the thing.

Taran · Ontario, Canada

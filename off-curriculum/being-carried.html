Being Carried.

On the quiet presence that arrives when there is nothing left of us to push.

A note ~5 min read

There is a thing most of us cannot say in serious company, but most of us have felt at least once.

Something carries us when we cannot carry ourselves. We do not always notice it...sometimes we only see it looking back, in the quiet of a different season. The way calm arrived when we had stopped reaching for it. The way the strength to keep moving was not, in the end, our strength. The way the worst night did not break us, even though we had no remaining force to refuse the breaking.

This is not the language of grit, motivation, or the push-through register that dominates the bookstores and the podcast feeds and the men's-development internet. That register is for people who still have something to push with. The experience this piece is naming arrives most clearly in the stretch when we have nothing left.

We are not motivated, not gritted, just emptied. And in the emptiness, something else carries the day. We come out the other side and we cannot honestly take credit for getting there.

The honest contemplative traditions have all named this. The Stoics called it Providence. The contemplative Christian tradition called it grace. The Tao Te Ching pointed at it without naming it. The Sufis call it tawakkul. The Sikh tradition opens its scripture with Ek Onkar, which translates as there is One. The names are different. The encounter is the same.

What gets felt is this. Something larger holds us, especially when we cannot hold ourselves. The larger thing is what each tradition gives a name to. Most of us encounter it in our own particular vocabulary, if we encounter it at all. Some of us encounter it without any vocabulary, in the silence after the surface life has been taken apart.

Marcus Aurelius, writing in the second century to himself in his private journal, addressed it as the Universe and called the relationship with it a kind of harmony:

Everything harmonizes with me, which is harmonious to thee, O Universe. Nothing for me is too early nor too late, which is in due time for thee. Marcus Aurelius · Meditations IV.23 · c. 170-180 CE · Long translation

He was a Roman emperor writing in Greek about a felt sense he could not push his way to. He was reminding himself, on the worst days, that what arrived had been prepared for him, and that he could trust it because he had felt the trust before.

This is not religion in the denominational sense. Many of us encountered the larger thing during the stretch when we were too exhausted to believe in any specific proposition about anything. It is not the result of belief. It is not the reward for moral effort. It does not have to be earned and it cannot be argued for in any way that will satisfy a skeptic.

It is also not the empty self-help register that uses the universe as a polite synonym for what I would like to happen. That is not what we are talking about. What we are talking about is older than that. The encounter has the felt quality of being met, not of getting what we wanted.

After we have felt it, we walk differently. We do not stop trying. We do not become passive. We just stop carrying what was never ours to carry.

The honest version, for most of us who have lived through a hard reset, is this. There was a stretch in the middle of the collapse when we were not motivated. There was nothing to be motivated by. And yet we did not collapse. Something carried us. We did not name it at the time. We did not need to. We just knew, in some quiet part of us, that we were not alone in the dark, even though every external measure said we were.

We come out of that stretch different. We can pretend the rebuild was our willpower if we want to. Many men do. But the honest reckoning, in the quiet, says something else carried us through the part we could not have carried ourselves.

What this piece wants to say is the smallest thing it can say. Something carried us. We know it because we are here, and the strength to get here did not, in the end, come only from us.

Something carries us.

We did not summon it.

We do not have to name it to know it was there.

The Hedonic Treadmill.

On the chase that doesn't deliver, and the way off the treadmill.

A note ~3 min read

We get the thing.

The job we worked for. The house we wanted. The income level that was supposed to take care of the worry. The relationship. The recognition. We hit the target we had been aiming at for years, and for a few weeks, maybe a few months, we feel different.

Then, quietly, we don't.

The baseline returns. The same low-grade dissatisfaction that drove the chase comes back, just attached to a new target. A bigger house. A better title. The next number. We move the goalpost without noticing, and we are running again, in the same direction, on what feels like new ground but is somehow the same ground.

This has a name. Psychologists call it the hedonic treadmill, and the findings are old and stubborn. Brickman and Campbell named it in 1971. A 1978 study compared lottery winners and people who had become paraplegic in accidents. Within a few months, the lottery winners had drifted back to roughly their pre-lottery baseline of happiness. The paraplegics, after a longer adjustment, were measurably closer to their old baseline than they had any reason to expect. External change moves the needle on inner satisfaction less than we think it will, and not for long.

The Stoics had the same observation two thousand years earlier, without the data. Seneca put it plainly:

It is not the man who has too little, but the man who craves more, that is poor. Seneca · Letters to Lucilius 2.6 · c. AD 65 · Campbell translation

Epicurus, often caricatured as a hedonist but actually closer to the Stoics on this, said the same thing from the other side:

If you wish to make Pythocles rich, do not give him more money, but diminish his desire. Epicurus · Vatican Sayings · c. 3rd century BCE

Both lines are getting at what the psychologists eventually confirmed. The chase is doing very little of what we think it is doing.

Here is what makes this hard to see from inside it.

The treadmill is doing its job. The reason we adapt back to baseline is the same reason our ancestors survived. We get used to whatever we have. The mind clears room for the next threat, the next opportunity. We adjust quickly to the good news and go looking for what is still missing. We are wired for it.

The cost is that, applied to a modern life of mostly-stable abundance, this same wiring keeps us chasing things that will not pay us back. We arrive at the thing, and the thing is now what we have, and what we have has stopped delivering the lift it was supposed to deliver, and so we set the next target and tell ourselves that will be the one.

It never is.

So what's the move.

The answer isn't to stop wanting things, and it isn't to retreat from ambition. It's to notice the treadmill while we are on it, and to put some of the work where it actually changes the experience instead of the surroundings.

The Stoics put it all under one heading. The work is internal. Composure. Attention. The practice of being present. The relationship to whatever you already have. None of that adapts away the way external goods do. The car adapts. The title adapts. The new house adapts. Your capacity to actually feel the day you are living in does not.

The practice is small. Notice when the chase is on. Notice when something you used to want has arrived and the dissatisfaction has somehow returned anyway. That is the treadmill, running. Look up. Find one thing you have that you stopped noticing six months ago, and spend a minute with it. Do it daily, on purpose. The treadmill keeps moving. You stop matching its speed.

The way off the treadmill is not to run faster.

It is to step off and look around.

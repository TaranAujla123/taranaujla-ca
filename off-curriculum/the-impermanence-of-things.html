The Impermanence of Things.

On the fact we keep forgetting, and what changes when we remember it.

A note ~3 min read

We forget that everything is moving.

The job we have is changing while we have it. The relationship we are in is changing while we are in it. The body that is reading this is not the body that read the same words a year ago, and will not be the body that reads them a year from now. The friend who has been a constant in our lives is one phone call away from a different story. The market we are succeeding in will not look the same in five years. The mood we feel right now will be gone by tomorrow morning, replaced by another.

None of this is breaking news. We know it intellectually. We forget it constantly.

The forgetting is the source of a lot of suffering. We hold things as if they were permanent, plan around them as if they were permanent, build identities on top of them as if they were permanent. Then they shift, as they were always going to, and we treat the shift as a personal violation. The shift was just the thing being what it was.

The Stoics had this nailed two thousand years ago. Marcus Aurelius, in a private notebook he kept to himself while running Rome, wrote it as plainly as anyone has:

Time is a sort of river of passing events, and strong is its current; no sooner is a thing brought to sight than it is swept by and another takes its place, and this too will be swept away. Marcus Aurelius · Meditations IV.43 · c. AD 170 · Long translation

He was running an empire when he wrote that. He was watching his own life pass and reminding himself that none of it was permanent. He wasn't complaining. He was getting clear.

Heraclitus, six hundred years before Marcus, said the same thing with a simpler picture:

You cannot step into the same river twice, for other waters are continually flowing on. Heraclitus · Fragment 91 · c. 500 BCE

Same point. The river you stepped into yesterday is not the river you are stepping into today. The water has moved on. So have you. The river is a name we give to a process we keep mistaking for a thing.

The Buddhists named the same observation centuries later. They called it anicca. The first of three marks of existence. Everything is impermanent. Every state we are in is on its way to becoming a different state.

Different cultures, same observation, separated by centuries. The thing we keep forgetting.

Here is what changes when we remember it.

We grip less. The deal that did not come through, the friendship that drifted, the season of life that ended, the version of ourselves we had to leave behind. These are the kind of losses we tend to read as wrongs done to us. Remembering impermanence puts them back in their actual category. They were always going to change. The change is not the violation. The expectation of permanence is the violation, and we are the ones who installed it.

We enjoy more. The day we are in, the person across from us, the food on the plate, the conversation we are having. None of these will be here in their current form in five years, or one year, or maybe one week. That is not a sad observation. It is the reason to actually be present for them now. We tend to take what looks permanent for granted, and almost everything looks more permanent than it is. The Stoics used the imagined loss of a thing as a practice to feel the thing more. Marcus would picture his children grown and gone. He would picture his own death. Then he would look at his actual life as it actually was and find it more vivid than before.

We let go better. When the time comes to release a thing (a job, a phase, a relationship, an identity, a chapter), the man who has been quietly remembering it was going to change all along has an easier time letting it move on. The one who has been treating it as permanent fights the change and prolongs the suffering. Same loss, two different relationships to it.

The practice is small.

A few times a week, look at something you have been treating as permanent. The work, the relationship, the body, the house, the routine, the role you play. Say to yourself plainly: this is going to change. Not as catastrophe, just as fact. Notice how saying it out loud shifts the way you hold the thing. The grip loosens. The thing gets a little more vivid. You hold it for what it is, which includes its eventual end.

Nothing we cling to was ever permanent.

The release of pretending otherwise is most of the freedom on offer.
